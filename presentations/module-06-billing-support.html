AWS Pricing Fundamentals

Understanding how AWS charges for services

We begin with the foundational pricing concepts. AWS pricing can seem complex, but it is built on three simple pillars. Understanding these pillars helps students reason about costs for any AWS service.

AWS Pricing Philosophy

Three pillars drive all AWS pricing decisions

Pay-As-You-Go

  • Pay only for what you consume
  • No upfront commitments
  • Scale up or down instantly
  • Convert fixed cost to variable cost

Save When You Reserve

  • Commit to 1-year or 3-year terms
  • Up to 72% discount vs. On-Demand
  • Available for EC2, RDS, ElastiCache, Redshift
  • Predictable workloads benefit most

Pay Less as You Use More

  • Volume-based discounts
  • Tiered pricing for storage
  • S3: lower per-GB rate at higher volumes
  • Data transfer discounts at scale
These three pillars are a common exam topic. Pay-as-you-go eliminates the need for capacity planning and upfront investment. Reservations reward commitment with significant discounts. Volume discounts reward scale. Ask students which pillar applies to their current workloads.

AWS Free Tier

Three types of free offers help you explore AWS at no cost

Always Free

  • Never expires
  • Lambda: 1M requests/month
  • DynamoDB: 25 GB storage
  • SNS: 1M publishes
  • CloudWatch: 10 custom metrics

12 Months Free

  • From account sign-up date
  • EC2: 750 hrs/month (t2.micro)
  • S3: 5 GB standard storage
  • RDS: 750 hrs/month (db.t2.micro)
  • CloudFront: 50 GB data out

Trials

  • Short-term from activation
  • SageMaker: 2 months free
  • Redshift: 2-month trial
  • Inspector: 15-day trial
  • Lightsail: 3 months free
Emphasize the distinction between these three tiers. Always Free services never expire regardless of account age. The 12-month offers are common exam traps -- students should know the clock starts at account creation, not first use. Trials vary by service. Warn students to monitor usage to avoid unexpected charges when free tier limits are exceeded.

Key Pricing Drivers

What Drives AWS Costs?

Nearly all AWS pricing can be broken down into three fundamental cost drivers:

Compute

  • Charged per hour or per second
  • Varies by instance type and size
  • OS and software licensing adds cost

Storage

  • Charged per GB per month
  • Varies by storage class/type
  • Provisioned vs. consumed models

Data Transfer

  • Inbound data: free
  • Outbound data: charged per GB
  • Inter-region transfers: charged both ways
These three drivers are the building blocks of almost every AWS bill. Compute is typically the largest cost for most organizations. Storage costs are usually modest but can grow quickly with data accumulation. Data transfer is often the most surprising cost -- especially inter-region and internet-bound traffic. Emphasize that inbound data transfer is always free.

EC2 Pricing Models

Model Commitment Discount Best For
On-Demand None None (baseline) Short-term, irregular, unpredictable workloads
Reserved Instances 1 year or 3 years Up to 72% Steady-state, predictable usage
Savings Plans 1 year or 3 years Up to 72% Flexible compute commitment (any region, family, OS)
Spot Instances None Up to 90% Fault-tolerant, flexible start/stop workloads
Dedicated Hosts None or Reserved Varies Compliance, licensing, regulatory requirements
This table is critical exam material. On-Demand is the default with no commitment. Reserved Instances offer the best discount for predictable workloads -- 1-year or 3-year terms with partial or full upfront payment options. Savings Plans are more flexible than RIs because they apply across instance families and regions. Spot Instances offer the deepest discounts but can be interrupted with 2-minute notice. Dedicated Hosts are for licensing or compliance needs. Ask students to identify which model fits different scenarios.

S3 Pricing Factors

Storage Class Rates

  • S3 Standard: highest availability, highest cost per GB
  • S3 Standard-IA: lower storage cost, retrieval fee applies
  • S3 Glacier: archival pricing, minutes to hours retrieval
  • S3 Glacier Deep Archive: lowest cost, 12-hour retrieval

Request and Transfer Pricing

  • PUT/COPY/POST: charged per 1,000 requests
  • GET/SELECT: charged per 1,000 requests (lower rate)
  • Data transfer out: per GB after first 100 GB/month
  • Transfer between regions: charged per GB

Key Insight: Choosing the right storage class based on access patterns is one of the most effective ways to reduce S3 costs. Use S3 Intelligent-Tiering for unpredictable access patterns.

S3 pricing has multiple dimensions: storage volume, storage class, number of requests, and data transfer. Students should understand that cheaper storage classes have retrieval costs or delays. S3 Intelligent-Tiering automatically moves objects between tiers based on access patterns -- useful when you cannot predict how often data will be accessed. Emphasize that lifecycle policies can automate transitions to cheaper classes over time.

Lambda Pricing

Serverless Pay-Per-Use Model

Lambda pricing is based on two dimensions:

Request Charges

  • $0.20 per 1 million requests
  • First 1M requests free (Always Free tier)
  • Each invocation counts as one request

Compute Duration

  • Measured in GB-seconds
  • Memory allocated x execution time
  • 400,000 GB-seconds free per month
  • Billed in 1ms increments

No charge when your code is not running -- true pay-per-use

Lambda pricing is straightforward and is a great example of pay-as-you-go. There is no charge for idle time, unlike EC2 where you pay for a running instance whether it is processing or not. The free tier for Lambda is generous: 1 million requests and 400,000 GB-seconds per month. Many lightweight workloads can run entirely within the free tier. GB-seconds means memory allocation multiplied by execution duration.

RDS and Database Pricing

Key Cost Components for Managed Databases

  • Instance Hours: based on instance class (db.t3.micro to db.r5.24xlarge), billed per hour
  • Storage: provisioned SSD (gp2/gp3) or Provisioned IOPS (io1), charged per GB/month
  • I/O Requests: for magnetic and Aurora I/O-Optimized, charged per million requests
  • Data Transfer: outbound data charged per GB; inbound and same-AZ traffic free

Remember: Multi-AZ deployments roughly double the instance and storage costs but provide high availability. Reserved Instances also apply to RDS for predictable database workloads.

RDS pricing mirrors EC2 in many ways -- instance hours, storage, and data transfer. The key difference is the I/O component, which matters for high-throughput databases. Multi-AZ is an important cost consideration: it doubles the infrastructure cost but is essential for production workloads. Aurora pricing differs slightly with per-I/O charges. RDS Reserved Instances offer the same discount structure as EC2 RIs.

Billing & Cost Management Tools

Visibility, control, and optimization for AWS spending

Now we move to the tools AWS provides to help you understand, track, and manage your costs. AWS offers a comprehensive suite of billing tools, each serving a different purpose. Understanding which tool to use in a given scenario is a frequent exam topic.

AWS Billing Dashboard

Your Central Billing Hub

The Billing Dashboard provides a high-level overview of your AWS spending:

  • Month-to-Date Spend: current charges broken down by service
  • Top Services: quick view of which services are driving costs
  • Forecasted Costs: projected end-of-month spend based on current usage
  • Free Tier Usage: track usage against free tier limits
  • Cost Trends: month-over-month comparison of spending
The Billing Dashboard is the first place to check for a quick understanding of your AWS costs. It is accessible from the AWS Management Console under Billing and Cost Management. Highlight the forecasted costs feature, which helps avoid surprises at the end of the month. The Free Tier Usage tracker is especially important for new accounts to avoid unexpected charges.

AWS Cost Explorer

Visualize and Analyze Your Costs

Cost Explorer provides detailed cost and usage data with powerful filtering and forecasting:

Key Features

  • Visualize costs over custom time ranges (daily, monthly)
  • Filter by service, region, account, or tag
  • Group by dimensions for drill-down analysis
  • View Reserved Instance utilization and coverage

Forecasting

  • Predict future spending using historical trends
  • Up to 12 months forward projection
  • Confidence intervals for estimates
  • Helps with budgeting and capacity planning
Cost Explorer is the primary tool for understanding where your money is going. It goes beyond the Billing Dashboard by allowing deep filtering and custom reports. The forecasting feature uses machine learning on historical data. Emphasize that Cost Explorer can reveal trends -- such as a service whose cost is gradually increasing -- that would be missed in a simple monthly view. Students should know this is distinct from Budgets, which is for setting alerts.

AWS Budgets

Set Custom Budgets

  • Define spending thresholds for cost, usage, reservation, or savings plans
  • Set monthly, quarterly, or annual budget periods
  • Track actual vs. budgeted amounts
  • Filter budgets by service, linked account, or tag

Alerts and Actions

  • Email and SNS notifications at custom thresholds (e.g., 80%, 100%)
  • Automated actions: apply IAM policy, stop EC2, apply SCP
  • Alerts on actual or forecasted amounts
  • Up to 5 alerts per budget

Four Budget Types: Cost budgets (dollar amounts), Usage budgets (service consumption), Reservation budgets (RI utilization), and Savings Plans budgets (commitment tracking).

AWS Budgets is the proactive cost management tool. While Cost Explorer shows you what happened, Budgets warns you before costs get out of control. Walk through a real scenario: setting a monthly budget of $500 with alerts at 80% and 100%. Mention that Budget Actions can automatically enforce cost controls, such as restricting resource creation via IAM policy. This is a key exam topic -- students should distinguish Budgets from Cost Explorer.

AWS Cost and Usage Reports (CUR)

The Most Detailed Cost Data Available

CUR provides the most comprehensive set of cost and usage data for your AWS account:

  • Line-item detail: every charge broken down by resource, operation, and pricing dimension
  • Delivered to S3: reports are automatically delivered to an S3 bucket on your schedule
  • Integration with analytics: query with Amazon Athena, visualize with QuickSight, or load into Redshift
  • Customizable: include resource IDs, split costs by usage type, hourly or daily granularity

CUR is the go-to tool when you need granular, programmatic access to billing data

CUR is the most detailed billing data source AWS offers. It is designed for organizations that need to build custom reports, run analysis in Athena, or feed data into third-party tools. It is not a console experience like Cost Explorer -- instead, it delivers CSV or Parquet files to S3. This is the answer when the exam asks about the most detailed or comprehensive billing data. It is commonly used by FinOps teams in large organizations.

AWS Pricing Calculator

Estimate Costs Before You Deploy

The AWS Pricing Calculator helps you model and estimate costs for new architectures:

  • Select services and configure resources to match your planned architecture
  • Compare pricing across regions and instance types
  • Generate shareable cost estimates via URL
  • Export estimates as CSV for procurement and budgeting
  • Available at calculator.aws -- no AWS account required

Exam Tip: When a question asks about estimating costs for a proposed architecture or migration, the answer is the AWS Pricing Calculator.

The Pricing Calculator is a web-based tool that does not require an AWS account. It replaced the older Simple Monthly Calculator. Walk students through a quick demo during the lab if time permits. Students should remember that this is the pre-deployment cost estimation tool, while Cost Explorer and Budgets are for tracking actual costs post-deployment.

Consolidated Billing with AWS Organizations

How It Works

  • Single payment method for all member accounts
  • Combined usage across accounts for volume discounts
  • One bill for the entire organization
  • Management account pays for all linked accounts

Benefits

  • Volume discounts: aggregate S3, EC2, data transfer usage
  • RI sharing: Reserved Instances apply across accounts
  • Detailed billing: per-account breakdowns still available
  • Free feature: no additional cost for consolidated billing

Example: If Account A uses 80 TB of S3 and Account B uses 60 TB, consolidated billing combines them to 140 TB, reaching a higher volume discount tier than either account alone.

Consolidated billing is a feature of AWS Organizations and is commonly tested on the exam. The key benefits are a single bill, volume discount aggregation, and RI sharing. Emphasize that each account still maintains its independence -- resources are not shared, only billing is consolidated. The volume discount example with S3 is a classic exam question format.

Cost Allocation Tags

Feature AWS-Generated Tags User-Defined Tags
Created By AWS automatically You define and apply them
Prefix aws: (e.g., aws:createdBy) user: (e.g., user:Project)
Activation Must be activated in Billing Console Must be activated in Billing Console
Use Cases Track who created resources Track by project, team, environment, cost center
Example aws:createdBy = iam-user/admin user:Environment = Production

Tags must be activated in the Billing Console before they appear in cost reports

Cost allocation tags are essential for organizations that need to track costs by project, department, or environment. Emphasize that tags must be activated in the Billing Console -- simply tagging a resource is not enough. A common best practice is to enforce tagging policies using AWS Organizations SCPs or AWS Config rules. Tags are retroactive only from the activation date forward, not for past usage.

AWS Cost Anomaly Detection

ML-Powered Spending Monitoring

  • Machine learning: automatically learns your spending patterns over time
  • Anomaly detection: identifies unusual spending that deviates from historical norms
  • Root cause analysis: pinpoints the service, account, region, or usage type causing the anomaly
  • Alerts: sends notifications via SNS or email when anomalies are detected
  • Customizable monitors: create monitors by AWS service, linked account, cost allocation tag, or cost category

No manual threshold required: Unlike Budgets, Cost Anomaly Detection uses ML to determine what is "unusual" based on your historical spending patterns.

Cost Anomaly Detection complements Budgets. While Budgets requires you to set a fixed threshold, Cost Anomaly Detection learns what normal looks like and alerts you when spending deviates. This is useful for catching unexpected cost spikes from misconfigurations, runaway resources, or security incidents. It is a free feature in the Cost Management console. Emphasize the distinction from Budgets on the exam.

AWS Support Plans

Choosing the right level of technical and account support

Support plans are a heavily tested topic on the Cloud Practitioner exam. Students need to know the five tiers, their key features, and which plan fits a given scenario. Pay special attention to response times and the TAM, which are common differentiators in exam questions.

AWS Support Plans Comparison

Feature Basic Developer Business Enterprise On-Ramp Enterprise
Price Free $29/mo or 3% of usage $100/mo or 10% of usage $5,500/mo $15,000/mo
Technical Support None Business-hours email 24/7 phone, email, chat 24/7 phone, email, chat 24/7 phone, email, chat
Response: Critical -- -- 1 hour 30 minutes 15 minutes
Trusted Advisor 7 core checks 7 core checks Full checks Full checks Full checks
TAM No No No Pool of TAMs Designated TAM
Concierge No No No No Yes
Walk through each tier carefully. Basic is included with every account. Developer is for testing and development. Business is the first tier with 24/7 support and full Trusted Advisor. Enterprise On-Ramp is a newer tier that provides a pool of TAMs at a lower price point than full Enterprise. Enterprise includes a designated TAM and a Concierge Support Team for billing and account questions. Response times for critical issues are a very common exam question.

Choosing the Right Support Plan

Basic: Individual learning or experimenting with AWS. Only documentation, whitepapers, and community forums for support.

Developer: Testing and development environments. Need general guidance during business hours but no production workloads.

Business: Production workloads in AWS. Need 24/7 access to Cloud Support Engineers and full Trusted Advisor checks.

Enterprise On-Ramp: Production and business-critical workloads. Need a pool of Technical Account Managers for proactive guidance.

Enterprise: Mission-critical workloads. Need a designated TAM, Concierge team, and 15-minute critical response time.

Frame each plan as a scenario-based decision. On the exam, questions typically describe a situation and ask which plan is appropriate. The key differentiators to remember: Developer is business-hours only. Business is the first 24/7 tier. Enterprise On-Ramp has pooled TAMs. Enterprise has a dedicated TAM and Concierge. If a question mentions a TAM, the answer is always Enterprise tier (On-Ramp or full).

AWS Trusted Advisor

Automated best-practice checks across five categories

Cost Optimization

  • Idle EC2 instances
  • Underutilized EBS volumes
  • Unassociated Elastic IPs

Performance

  • High-utilization instances
  • CloudFront optimization
  • EC2 to EBS throughput

Security

  • Open security groups
  • IAM access key rotation
  • MFA on root account

Fault Tolerance

  • EBS snapshots age
  • Multi-AZ RDS
  • Auto Scaling groups

Service Limits

  • VPC limits
  • EC2 instance limits
  • IAM policy limits
Trusted Advisor is an automated inspection tool that evaluates your AWS environment against best practices. All accounts get 7 core checks covering basic security items like S3 bucket permissions and MFA on root. Business and Enterprise plans unlock the full set of checks across all five categories. The five categories are a very common exam question. Remember the mnemonic: Cost, Performance, Security, Fault tolerance, Service limits.

Trusted Advisor: Checks by Plan Tier

Basic and Developer Plans

7 Core Checks:

  • S3 bucket permissions
  • Security groups (unrestricted ports)
  • IAM use (at least one IAM user)
  • MFA on root account
  • EBS public snapshots
  • RDS public snapshots
  • Service limits

Business and Enterprise Plans

Full Suite of Checks:

  • All 7 core checks plus
  • Cost optimization recommendations
  • Performance improvement suggestions
  • Additional security checks
  • Fault tolerance assessments
  • Programmatic access via API
  • CloudWatch integration for alerts
The distinction between core checks and full checks is important for the exam. If a question asks about cost optimization checks in Trusted Advisor, the answer requires at least a Business support plan. The 7 core checks focus on security and service limits. Programmatic access via the AWS Support API is also limited to Business and Enterprise tiers.

Health Dashboards

AWS Personal Health Dashboard

  • Personalized view of service health
  • Shows events affecting your resources
  • Proactive notifications for scheduled maintenance
  • Remediation guidance for affected resources
  • EventBridge integration for automated responses

AWS Service Health Dashboard

  • General view of all AWS service status
  • Shows current and historical outages
  • Region-by-region service availability
  • Public RSS feeds for status updates
  • No account login required

Key Difference: Personal Health Dashboard shows events relevant to your specific resources. Service Health Dashboard shows the status of all AWS services globally.

The exam frequently asks about the difference between these two dashboards. The Personal Health Dashboard is the answer when a question mentions being notified about events affecting your specific resources or accounts. The Service Health Dashboard is the answer for general AWS service status. The Personal Health Dashboard is now integrated into the AWS Health Dashboard in the console. EventBridge integration allows automated remediation when issues are detected.

Cost Optimization Strategies

Practical approaches to reducing your AWS bill

This section pulls together everything we have covered into actionable cost optimization strategies. These strategies are both exam-relevant and practically valuable. Cost optimization is one of the five pillars of the Well-Architected Framework covered in the previous module.

Right-Sizing Instances

Match Instance Type to Workload Requirements

  • Analyze utilization: use CloudWatch metrics to identify instances running below 40% CPU or memory utilization
  • Downsize over-provisioned instances: move from m5.xlarge to m5.large if metrics support it
  • Change instance families: switch from general-purpose to compute- or memory-optimized based on workload profile
  • Use AWS Compute Optimizer: ML-driven recommendations for optimal instance types based on actual usage

Common Mistake: Choosing the largest instance "just in case." Over-provisioning is one of the top sources of wasted cloud spend.

Right-sizing is the first and most impactful cost optimization strategy. Many organizations migrate to the cloud with the same sizing mentality as on-premises, leading to massive over-provisioning. AWS Compute Optimizer analyzes CloudWatch metrics and recommends optimal instance types. This should be an ongoing process, not a one-time exercise. Mention that Trusted Advisor also flags underutilized instances.

Reserved Instances and Savings Plans

Reserved Instances

  • Best for steady-state workloads
  • Standard RIs: up to 72% discount, limited flexibility
  • Convertible RIs: up to 66% discount, can change instance attributes
  • Payment options: All Upfront, Partial Upfront, No Upfront

Savings Plans

  • Commit to a consistent $/hour of compute usage
  • Compute Savings Plans: apply across EC2, Lambda, Fargate
  • EC2 Instance Savings Plans: specific to instance family and region
  • More flexible than RIs, same discount levels

Strategy: Start with Savings Plans for flexibility, then layer in Standard RIs for workloads you are confident will not change.

This slide deepens the earlier pricing models discussion with optimization strategy. The key decision is between RIs and Savings Plans. Savings Plans are generally recommended for new commitments because they offer more flexibility. The discount is applied automatically to matching usage. All Upfront payment gives the highest discount, No Upfront gives the least. A common exam scenario presents a workload description and asks which commitment model provides the best savings.

Additional Optimization Strategies

Spot Instances

  • Up to 90% off On-Demand
  • Ideal for batch processing, CI/CD, data analysis
  • Use Spot Fleet for availability
  • Handle interruptions gracefully

S3 Lifecycle Policies

  • Automate transitions between storage classes
  • Move to Standard-IA after 30 days
  • Archive to Glacier after 90 days
  • Delete expired objects automatically

Monitoring and Alerts

  • Set AWS Budgets with alert thresholds
  • Enable Cost Anomaly Detection
  • Review Trusted Advisor weekly
  • Use Cost Explorer for trends

Cost optimization is an ongoing process, not a one-time activity

These three strategies complement right-sizing and reservations. Spot Instances are powerful for workloads that can tolerate interruptions -- batch jobs, rendering, testing. S3 Lifecycle Policies automate the process of moving data to cheaper tiers as it ages. Monitoring ties everything together: you cannot optimize what you do not measure. The key takeaway is that cost optimization requires continuous attention and iteration. Encourage students to establish a regular review cadence for their AWS costs.

Key Takeaways

  • Three Pricing Pillars: Pay-as-you-go, save when you reserve, and pay less as you use more form the foundation of all AWS pricing.
  • EC2 Pricing Models: On-Demand for flexibility, Reserved/Savings Plans for steady workloads (up to 72% savings), Spot for fault-tolerant jobs (up to 90% savings).
  • Billing Tools: Use Cost Explorer for analysis, Budgets for alerts, CUR for granular data, and the Pricing Calculator for pre-deployment estimates.
  • Support Plans: Basic is free, Business is first 24/7 tier, Enterprise includes a designated TAM and 15-minute critical response.
  • Trusted Advisor: Five categories of automated best-practice checks; full checks require Business or Enterprise support.
  • Cost Optimization: Right-size instances, leverage commitments, use Spot where possible, automate lifecycle policies, and monitor continuously.
